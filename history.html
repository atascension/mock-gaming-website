The Journey

Every game has a story.
Every console has a legacy.

The Timeline

1970s

The Arcade Era

Before the home console existed, gaming lived in darkened arcades — a world of blinking screens, chunky joysticks, and the relentless clatter of quarters. Pong launched the industry in 1972. Space Invaders caused a national coin shortage in Japan. Pac-Man became a cultural phenomenon that transcended gaming entirely.

  • Pong (Atari, 1972)
  • Space Invaders (Taito, 1978)
  • Asteroids (Atari, 1979)
  • Pac-Man (Namco, 1980)
  • Donkey Kong (Nintendo, 1981)
1980s

The 8-Bit Era

Gaming came home. The Nintendo Entertainment System resurrected a crashed industry in North America, while the Atari 2600 and Commodore 64 built massive followings of their own. Entire childhoods were defined by the games of this era — and millions of those cartridges are still out there, waiting to be found.

  • Atari 2600 (1977)
  • Commodore 64 (1982)
  • NES / Famicom (1983 / 1985)
  • Sega Master System (1985)
  • Game Boy (1989)
Early 1990s

The 16-Bit Era

The console wars arrived in full force. SNES vs. Sega Genesis divided playgrounds, sparked arguments, and produced some of the greatest games ever made. Sonic ran. Mario flew. Streets of Rage brawled. Chrono Trigger told a story that still moves people decades later. This era defined what gaming could be as an art form.

  • Sega Genesis / Mega Drive (1989)
  • Super Nintendo (SNES, 1990)
  • TurboGrafx-16 (1987 Japan)
  • Neo Geo (1990)
Mid 1990s

The 32/64-Bit Era

Three dimensions changed everything. Sony’s original PlayStation arrived and rewrote the rules of the industry. The Nintendo 64 delivered Mario in 3D for the first time, a moment that felt genuinely historic. The Sega Saturn competed fiercely and left behind a cult legacy. CD-ROM opened the door to cinematic storytelling and full audio soundtracks.

  • Sega Saturn (1994)
  • Sony PlayStation (1994)
  • Nintendo 64 (1996)
Early 2000s

The Early 2000s

The final generation that many collectors consider “retro.” The PlayStation 2 became the best-selling console of all time. The GameCube built a passionate following despite finishing third in its generation. The original Xbox brought PC-level power to the living room. And the Game Boy Advance quietly delivered a stunning library of 2D masterpieces.

  • PlayStation 2 (2000)
  • Game Boy Advance (2001)
  • Nintendo GameCube (2001)
  • Xbox (2001)

Console Spotlights

Atari 2600
Image
1977

Atari 2600

The console that brought the arcade into the living room. With interchangeable cartridges and a library spanning hundreds of titles, the 2600 defined an era and introduced millions to home gaming.

Key Games: Pitfall, Combat, Adventure, Space Invaders

Commodore 64
Image
1982

Commodore 64

The best-selling personal computer of all time. The C64’s SID chip produced sounds unlike anything else in the era. A generation of European gamers grew up on its impossibly deep library.

Key Games: Impossible Mission, Turrican, Maniac Mansion

NES
Image
1985

Nintendo Entertainment System

The console that saved the North American video game industry. Mario, Zelda, Metroid, Mega Man — the NES didn’t just sell hardware, it created franchises that still define gaming today.

Key Games: Super Mario Bros., The Legend of Zelda, Mega Man 2

Sega Genesis
Image
1989

Sega Genesis

Blast processing. Attitude. The Genesis went head-to-head with the SNES and carved out a loyal, passionate fanbase that persists to this day. Sonic the Hedgehog became gaming’s second great mascot.

Key Games: Sonic the Hedgehog, Streets of Rage, Mortal Kombat

SNES
Image
1990

Super Nintendo (SNES)

Widely considered to have the greatest software library in gaming history. Every genre peaked on the SNES — platformer, RPG, fighting game, action adventure. An almost impossibly strong machine.

Key Games: Super Mario World, Chrono Trigger, A Link to the Past

PlayStation
Image
1994

Sony PlayStation

Sony entered the console market and changed it permanently. The PS1 brought 3D gaming, CD-quality audio, and a new era of cinematic storytelling. Over 100 million units sold. A landmark machine.

Key Games: Final Fantasy VII, Metal Gear Solid, Crash Bandicoot

Nintendo 64
Image
1996

Nintendo 64

The console that proved 3D gaming could be joyful. Super Mario 64 and Ocarina of Time didn’t just define the N64 — they defined entire genres and remain benchmark titles 30 years later.

Key Games: Super Mario 64, Ocarina of Time, GoldenEye 007
